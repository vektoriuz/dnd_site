Character Sheet Guide

This guide walks you through the process of filling out your character sheet step by step. It assumes you have already chosen your class, background, and species.

Follow the steps in order. Each section builds on the previous one and completes a different part of your character’s mechanical profile.

Ability Scores

Begin by determining your six ability scores. These form the foundation of your character’s capabilities and many of other details on your character sheet depend on them.

  • Strength
  • Dexterity
  • Constitution
  • Intelligence
  • Wisdom
  • Charisma

Assign Ability Scores

First, determine the basic set of values. The DM should decide the exact way how to do it. Below are two of the most basic ways, adhering to the balance. These two ways are compatible with each other, so different players can choose whichever suits them better. There are other ways to generate scores, like random dice rolls, but those may result in an unbalanced scores distributions among players.

The most straightforward way is to use the predetermined score points from the Standard Array. This way is the best for players who don't want to deal with this step too much and still have an effective points distribution for their class.

Adjust Ability Scores

After assigning your ability scores, adjust them according to your background. Your background lists three abilities; increase one of those by 2 and a different one by 1, or increase all three by 1. None of these increases can raise a score above 20.

Some players like to increase their class's primary ability, while others prefer to increase a low score.

Ability Modifiers

Each ability score provides a modifier, which is used in most rolls. These modifiers will affect attacks, saving throws, skills, and many class features.

Record the modifier for each ability score on your sheet using the Ability Scores and Modifiers table.

Proficiencies

Your proficiencies come from your class and background.

This includes:

  • Saving throw proficiencies (from your class)
  • Skill proficiencies (from class and background)
  • Tool proficiencies (from background or features)
  • Weapon and armor proficiencies (from your class)

Mark all applicable proficiencies on your character sheet.

For Weapons and tools, list all proficiencies in the respective sections on your character sheet.

Skills values

Assign value to each skill.

  • For a skill without proficiency, copy the value from the associated ability modifier.
  • For a skill with proficiency, add together the value from the associated ability modifier and proficiency bonus.

The proficiency bonus is determined by your character level. For details, see the Proficiency Bonus by Character Level table.

Derived Statistics

Several key values are calculated based on your class and ability scores.

  • Armor Class: Without armor or shield, AC is 10 + Dexterity modifier. Otherwise, follow your equipment description.
  • Hit Points: For Max HP, add together your character class hit die maximum number and Constitution modifier. At the start, set the current HP equal to Max HP. Leave Temp alone.
  • Hit Dice: At level 1, you get one dice of the type according to your class Core Traits. For each new level, your max dice number increases by one.
  • Initiative: Fill in your Dexterity modifier.
  • Speed: Primarily determined by your character's species. Can be modified via various other effects.
  • Size: Determined by your character's species.
  • Passive Perception: 10 + perception modifier (proficiency bonus is also applied if proficient in Perception).

Record these values on your sheet.

Weapons and Damage Cantrips

Write here your starting weapons. The attack roll bonus for a weapon with which you have proficiency is one of the following, unless a weapon's property says otherwise:

  • Melee attack bonus = Strength Modifier + Proficiency Bonus
  • Ranged attack bonus = Dexterity Modifier + Proficiency Bonus

Look up the damage and properties of your weapons in Equipment page. You add the same ability modifieryou use for attacks with a weapon to your damage rolls with that weapon.

Chose Languages

Your character knows at least three languages: Common plus two languages you roll on or choose from the Standard Languages table. Knowledge of a language means your character can communicate in it, read it and write it. Your class and other features might also give you languages.

The Standard Languages table lists languages that are widespread on D&D worlds. Every player character knows Common, which originated in the planar metropolis of Sigil, the hub of the multiverse. The other standard languages originated with the first mambers of the most prominent species in the worlds of D&D and have since spread widely.

The Rare Languages table lists languages that are either secret or derived from other planes of existence and thus less widespread in the worlds of the Material Plane. Some features let a character learn a rare language.

Completion

You can now use it as your primary reference during the game.
